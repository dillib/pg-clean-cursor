Why yellow as the silhouette

The smile alone asked the yellow to do all the work in a 4-pixel-wide zone — it was a nice touch but not an identity. Filling the whole silhouette makes the brand readable at a glance from any distance, and lets the mark act as its own badge or spot-colour block in layout.

Why keep the ink version

A one-colour fallback is non-negotiable for real-world use: etched into metal hang-tags, laser-scored into leather, single-ink printed on thermal receipts, or sitting at favicon sizes. Same geometry, same smile, same story — just no colour.

Why the smile becomes ink

Inside the yellow silhouette, three yellow smile-dots on a yellow body would disappear. Flipping the smile and eyes to ink keeps the face legible without introducing any third colour. Still a strict two-colour palette: yellow + ink.

What stays the same

Every proportion from v1 carries over: 4:5 body, 16-px pentagonal head, 4-px grid, r=2 string-hole, the exact eye and smile coordinates. The tag is still a tag. It just wears its colour out loud now.

✓ Do
  • Use the yellow tag as the default — it's the primary mark.
  • Swap to the ink tag below 20 px, on yellow backgrounds, or when printing single-ink.
  • Keep eyes and smile ink inside the yellow body. Same two colours, always.
  • Pair with the wordmark at the same cap-height — tag body = wordmark x-height × 1.8.
✗ Don't
  • Outline the silhouette — solid fill only, never a stroke.
  • Put the yellow tag on a yellow background. Use the ink tag there.
  • Introduce a third colour (paper eyes, coloured smile, gradients). Two colours, full stop.
  • Change the 4:5 proportion, the eye positions, or the smile curve.
